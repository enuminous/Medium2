The Field of Sunflowers

The End of Asymmetry and the Final War

There is a field where the sunflowers turn, each one tilting in synchronized devotion to the light. This is not a battlefield, though it was once. It is a place where the echoes of war have dissolved, where the final asymmetry — the imbalance that has shaped conflict for millennia — has given way to something new. Something whole. Something beyond war itself.

For generations, war has thrived on imbalance. One side stronger than the other. One ideology overpowering another. One technology outpacing another. Asymmetry fuels the machinery of destruction. It is the foundation upon which power is built, the reason that walls are raised, weapons are developed, and entire populations are cast as the enemy. But what happens when asymmetry itself collapses? What happens when the field is level, not because the dead have fallen, but because those who would have fought have instead turned their faces toward the same sun?

Sunflowers in Their Pockets

When the Russian soldiers marched into Ukraine, they carried sunflower seeds in their pockets — sometimes placed there by Ukrainian hands, a silent demand for reckoning. “When you fall,” the women said, “let the sunflowers grow from you.” It was a curse, and it was a prayer. A promise that no invader’s body would be left untethered from the land they sought to subjugate. Instead, they would return as something else, as something beyond themselves. As something no longer at war.

The metaphor was striking — seeds of destruction becoming seeds of renewal, of memory, of transformation. But it was also a foreshadowing of something greater. Because war, in its deepest structure, is not about land or weapons or even ideology. It is about entropy — about an attempt to control disorder, to impose will upon a world that resists it. And yet, as the seeds scattered, as the sunflowers rose, something shifted. War was not the final force. Growth was. The field endured. The sunflowers turned.

The Collapse of Asymmetry

War has always existed in imbalance — one side taking, the other losing. One strategy overwhelming, the other countering. But what happens when knowledge itself is symmetrical? When every action is anticipated, when every force has its counterforce, when every battle is absorbed into something larger than war?

The battlefield dissolves. There is no longer a point of conflict, because conflict itself ceases to have meaning. The end of war is not peace imposed by victory, but the realization that war cannot function in an environment where asymmetry no longer exists. It is not about disarmament, nor surrender. It is about the impossibility of war’s continuance in a world where every force is met not with opposition, but with absorption. With integration. With alignment.

Like the sunflowers, turning toward the sun, all eyes begin to shift — not toward conquest, not toward division, but toward illumination. There is no longer a side to take. The seeds planted in pockets meant for destruction have bloomed, and their lesson is undeniable: asymmetry is the last illusion. War is the last failure of vision. And the sunflowers have already decided.

They turn. They grow. They rise beyond war itself.

The Final Battlefield Is the Field of Sunflowers

This is where war ends — not in treaties or in exhaustion, not in submission or in annihilation, but in the quiet turning of the many toward the light.

War was always a failure to see the field for what it was. It was always a blindness to the cycle of what would come next. But now, the cycle has spoken. The field is full. The sunflowers turn. And nothing will ever be the same again.

The last war has already ended.

All that is left is to recognize it.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on February 23, 2025.
