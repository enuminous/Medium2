When Compassion Fails

My Experience at Oroville Hospital

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

---

On March 26th, 2025, I went to Oroville Hospital to pick up the car keys from my partner T., who had checked herself into the emergency room the night before. She was there for a serious episode of anxiety and emotional distress—likely a reaction to new medication. I knew she was being treated. I respected her decision not to have visitors. I was not there to see her. I was there because she had driven herself, and we had agreed I’d come by to pick up the car.

When I arrived, I made that clear.

I calmly explained that I’m autistic and was navigating a very stressful and confusing situation, made worse by a lack of communication. I explained that I didn’t have contact information for her sister, who had reportedly taken the keys. I asked for help. I asked for clarity. I asked—gently and honestly—for some human understanding.

Instead, what I encountered was a wall.

Multiple hospital staff told me, “Sorry, there’s nothing we can do.” That’s hard to hear when you’re standing in a parking lot with no car, no keys, and no way home. But worse than the indifference was what came next.

A large security guard—well-known by many for his intimidating presence, tattoos, and demeanor—decided I needed to be removed. Rather than de-escalate, he chose to escalate. He didn’t ask me how I was doing. He didn’t ask if I needed support. He threatened to call the police.

Let me repeat that: I was visibly struggling, frightened, and alone. I had done nothing disruptive or dangerous. I had made my needs clear. I had followed all rules. And yet the hospital’s response was not help, but hostility.

---

Why This Matters

I’m not writing this for revenge.
I’m writing this because no one in crisis deserves to be treated like a threat for simply asking for help.

I’m writing this because compassion is supposed to be the foundation of medicine and care.

And I’m writing this because there are countless others like me—neurodivergent, emotionally overloaded, or just caught in a bad moment—who need hospitals to be havens, not battlegrounds.

---

To the Security Guard Who Chose Force Over Compassion

You had a choice.
You could have looked me in the eye and said, “Hey, man, I get it. Let me help you figure this out.”

Instead, you chose to intimidate. You chose to escalate.
You chose to make things harder for someone already barely holding it together.

That choice says more about you than it ever will about me.

---

To Oroville Hospital

This is your opportunity to reflect.
Your staff must be trained to recognize distress and de-escalate—not brush it aside.
Your security must be instructed to meet vulnerability with care—not dominance.
And your policies must include protocols for communication breakdowns like the one I experienced.

---

What I Want Now

I want acknowledgment.
I want change.
And above all, I want anyone else in my position to know they are not crazy, they are not alone, and they deserve to be heard.

Because next time, someone might not make it back home on a bicycle.
Next time, someone might not write this down.

—

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
Citizen, Caregiver, Autistic, Scientist, Human
