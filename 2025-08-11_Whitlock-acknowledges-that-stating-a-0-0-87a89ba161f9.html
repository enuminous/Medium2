Whitlock acknowledges that stating a 0.0 error margin—absolute certainty—is unrealistic for experimental measurements. He allows it might hold in “certain theoretical or mathematical truths.” So far, that’s accurate.

But here’s the key problem: He blurs the line between mathematical ideal and physical reality, implying that because a theoretical assertion could, in principle, be exact, his “Motion‑First Physics” can claim perfect precision in describing the universe.

Where the Physics Breaks

Measurement limits are not optional.

In quantum physics, the uncertainty principle—a fundamental constraint—mandates that you cannot know both position and momentum precisely. It’s not just a practical limit; it’s a built-in property of nature.

Measurement itself distorts the system.

The phenomenon of quantum back-action tells us that any attempt to measure a property inherently changes it. That makes zero-error not just implausible—it’s physically impossible at the micro level.

Zero-point energy complicates the ideal vacuum.

Even in what seems like "empty space," zero-point fluctuations mean there’s an irreducible background energy—so the ideal baseline is itself noisy.

Summary of the Error

Whitlock’s Claim / Real Physics Constraint

“Zero error is possible in theoretical truth” / Quantum uncertainty prohibits perfect knowledge.

Suggests physics can be derived with perfect, absolute precision.

Back-action and zero-point effects invalidate that level of exactness in measurement.

In short: claiming zero-error margins in physics, even in theory, sidesteps fundamental limits of nature. This isn’t just metaphoric — it’s a misapplication of theory to facts.
