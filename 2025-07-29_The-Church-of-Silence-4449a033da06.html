The Church of Silence

What They Did to My Grandmother, What They Allowed to Happen to My Father, and Why I Will Never Forgive Them.

I remember the smell.

Old dust. Polished wood. The ghost of perfume meant to sweeten what couldn’t be sanctified.

That’s what it was like walking into the Church of Christ, Scientist in Davis, California—the place that had taken my grandmother when I was still a boy. The place that had welcomed her collapse with silence. The place that stood by as my father, disheveled and undone, wandered back in half a century later, broken and muttering, and they let him sit there like a relic—with crumbs in his beard, his eyes vacant, and no one even bothering to ask, “Sir, are you alright? Should we call someone?”

They didn’t lift a finger.

They just sat in their pressed Sunday clothing, murmuring Baker Eddy’s scripted verses, while my father fell apart in real time five feet away.
And they called it grace.

They had done the same to my grandmother, June Wright, decades earlier.
When her husband, Sergeant Harold Lewis Wright of the California Highway Patrol, vanished from the public record—erased after a mysterious heart attack while on duty, likely connected to secrets he carried from his time protecting Governor Earl Warren—June broke under the weight of silence.

She began to ask questions.
She began to remember.
She began, perhaps, to speak the truth.

And that’s when the Church took her away.

Her friends—the Christ Scientists—stepped in.
Not to comfort. Not to hold.
But to remove.

Because in their eyes, grief wasn’t real. Trauma wasn’t real. Death was illusion.
Pain was error.
And truth was the greatest heresy of all.

They had her institutionalized in a religious hospital.
I didn’t see her for nearly a decade.

I was just starting to ask questions.

The theology is polished, but the damage is primal.

This is a church that tells people the body isn’t real, sickness is a mistake of the mind, and psychiatric care is a betrayal of divine law.
They have let children die without medical care.
They have let mothers suffer in silence.
They have wrapped grief in scripture and called it healing while the soul bleeds out, unseen.

They took my grandmother’s voice.
They watched my father unravel.
They failed him in 2021 just as they failed her in 1971 in a mid $5-figure pseudo-psychology swindle.

I will never forgive them.
Not because of their faith, but because of what they did with it.

They were gatekeepers of memory, and when memory threatened their perfectionist theology, they chose to let the people who remembered fall into ruin.

That church taught me that the worst kind of evil doesn’t come with fangs or flame.
It comes with polite silence, and clean clothes, and a refusal to pick up the phone while a man forgets his own name three feet away.

They let my father decay in their pews.
They let my grandmother vanish behind theological bars.
And they almost got me, too.

But not anymore.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on July 29, 2025.
