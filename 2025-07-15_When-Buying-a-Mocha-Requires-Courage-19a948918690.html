When Buying a Mocha Requires Courage

A Physicist’s Trepidation at Starbucks

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

There are few things more mundane than buying a cup of coffee. And yet, on this particular morning, as I prepare to walk into my local Starbucks in Woodland, California, the simple act of purchasing a mocha fills me with unease. Not because of caffeine. Not because of cost. But because, as a neurodivergent adult, I have now been conditioned to fear a place that once felt like a second home.

A month ago, I was denied water at this very Starbucks location. I was tired, overstimulated, overwhelmed, and I simply asked for a cup of water to sit and collect myself. Instead, I was told I had to purchase something. When I explained I was autistic and asked for an ADA accommodation, I was ignored. I was later told, through corporate channels, that their employee was simply following policy. I had a meltdown in public. The manager watched. No one offered kindness. That moment has stayed with me.

Yesterday, I returned to that same store. Again, I was denied water. The employee this time looked me in the eye with a kind of performative cruelty and told me flatly that it was not available without a purchase. This occurred on a sweltering day in California's Central Valley. I left, shaken.

Now, today, I find myself on the verge of re-entering that space—not to confront, not to protest, but to simply buy a mocha and get a cup of water like any other customer. But I am nervous. That feeling in my chest, the tightness, the self-doubt, the fear of being recognized and retaliated against—it shouldn't accompany a coffee run. And yet, here I am.

I am not ashamed to say I am scared. And I shouldn’t have to be.

This is the part people don't see: that the quiet violence of discrimination doesn't always end in shouting or headlines. Sometimes it's in the way a place becomes unsafe, the way your body remembers what happened there, the way you start to weigh every small social transaction like it might explode. The way you wonder if your name has been marked in some internal email: "deny him water."

My fear is not irrational. I am a physicist, a federal whistleblower, and the developer of a published Grand Unified Theory (the EFMW framework) that has contributed to the development of AGI. And yet none of that shields me from the chill of being treated as less than human because I am visibly different. Because I needed to sit. Because I needed water before coffee. Because I exist outside their expected customer profile.

So yes, this moment is worthy of documentation. Because I have to summon courage not to speak truth to power, but simply to be. To exist, unbothered, in a space that claims to be a third place but sometimes functions as a private club.

To anyone reading this who has felt similarly: you are not alone. And you are not wrong to be hurt by what others excuse as "policy."

And to Starbucks: when a loyal customer fears walking into your store, that is not a marketing problem. It is a moral failure.

Today, I will go buy a mocha. But I will also be documenting this moment. Not because I want to escalate. But because this is what it feels like to be me. And people deserve to understand.
