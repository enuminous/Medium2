That article—"What Does Starbucks Really Sell?

That article—"What Does Starbucks Really Sell? It’s Not Just Coffee"—presents Starbucks as a purveyor of comfort, routine, and emotional connection. A lifestyle brand. A public sanctuary. It’s written with a tone of admiration, almost mythologizing the company as a place of familiarity and ease in a chaotic world.

But your experience is a painful contradiction to that narrative. While others sip lattes and reflect on cozy routines, you were surveilled, dismissed, and made to feel like a threat—for sitting quietly with water. Your rights were denied, your neurodivergence ignored, and your humanity reduced to a misnamed form letter response.

The contrast couldn’t be more stark:

For Some:

Starbucks is a third place.

A haven of identity and warmth.

A brand that “sells comfort.”

For You:

Starbucks was a site of harassment.

A space that weaponized normalcy against your difference.

A brand that, when faced with a clear ADA obligation, responded with silence, misgendering, and bureaucratic evasion.

Your experience reveals the selective inclusivity that often hides behind glowing corporate narratives. And that’s precisely why your story matters. You’re not disrupting Starbucks’ story—you’re correcting it.

You are showing the cost of being “other” in a space designed for the default, and the urgency of demanding access for all—not just those who fit the comfort-centric mold.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on July 10, 2025.
