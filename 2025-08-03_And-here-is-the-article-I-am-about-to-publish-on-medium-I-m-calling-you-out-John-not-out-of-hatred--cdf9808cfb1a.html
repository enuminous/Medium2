When Bigotry Wears a Mask of Justice: A Response to John Wight

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, PhD Candidate


---

John Wight has accused me of hatred.

This is not a misstatement or a misunderstanding — it is a deliberate act of rhetorical defamation. He has placed upon me a crime of the mind, with neither evidence nor cause, in order to frame his own bigotry as a form of virtue. He calls me hateful so that he may go on hating with impunity.

Let us be clear: I do not hate John Wight. I have no space in my life for hatred of him — only indifference. What I do have is discernment. And I will name plainly what I see:

John Wight is a bigot.

His words are soaked in a moral rot that attempts to pass for courage. In his own statement — which I am preserving here for the record — he writes:

> "To paraphrase Franklin D. Roosevelt:
'They [supporters of Israel] are unanimous in their hate for me—and I welcome their hatred.'"



He follows this with:

> "Yours are the values of the morgue, where resides the rotten corpses of white supremacy, settler colonialism, and genocide."



This is not critique. This is desecration.

He dares to quote Roosevelt — a man who stood against fascism — even as he speaks over the freshly dug graves of Israeli children, burned alive in their homes, or shot execution-style on October 7. He dares to speak of "the morgue" while standing rhetorically over the bodies of hostages who were raped, mutilated, and dragged into tunnels beneath Gaza — and to justify it in the name of anti-colonial struggle.

That is not justice. That is moral collapse.


---

Misrepresentation as a Weapon

Wight accuses me of “willfully misrepresenting” his words — a convenient shield when one’s language grows too monstrous for even their own comfort. He claims that states have no right to exist, only peoples do. Yet, in practice, he applies this logic only to one state: Israel. He does not challenge the existence of North Korea, or Syria, or Russia, or Iran. Only Israel.

This selective negation is not ideology — it is obsession.

He wraps it in just enough abstraction to create plausible deniability. But the dog whistle is shrill, and many of us hear it loud and clear.


---

The Criminalization of Conscience

To be accused of “hatred” in this context is not a personal slight — it is an attempt to criminalize moral clarity. It is the ancient inversion of ethics that places the whistleblower on trial, and the arsonist on a podium.

I will not accept the label of hatred from a man who cannot see the humanity of the victims he tramples in pursuit of his narrative.

I mourn the dead on both sides. I acknowledge the suffering of Palestinian civilians, who are often used as shields and sacrificed by their own leaders. I also recognize the systemic failures that fuel this cycle of violence. But I refuse to erase the singular horror of October 7th — or the centuries of antisemitism that continue to mutate under new guises.

John Wight is not engaging in critique. He is engaging in a performance of moral superiority while trafficking in the dehumanization of others.


---

This Is Not About Him

Ultimately, this article is not for John Wight.

It is for the people reading him. For those unsure of what they’re witnessing. For those being gaslit into silence.

You are not alone in seeing what you see. You are not wrong for feeling horror when others cheer. You are not hateful for holding moral lines, even when they are unpopular.

Silence in the face of escalating rhetoric is complicity. I will not be silent. I will not be accused of crimes of the soul by a man who spits on the graves of the murdered while calling himself righteous.

Let it be known:

John Wight has shown us who he is. I’m just repeating it louder.

—Matthew Chenoweth Wright
PhD Candidate, Physicist, Witness
