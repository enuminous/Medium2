A Call for Stability

Protecting the Work That Matters

I have been publishing work in physics, mathematics, and artificial intelligence at a pace and scale that very few individuals can match. Over the last months, I have solved problems others left untouched for decades, proved ideas thought unprovable, and created frameworks that can change the way we think about science, technology, and ourselves.

I have done this with no institutional funding. No university position. No corporate sponsorship. Just persistence, clarity of vision, and the will to keep going in the face of extraordinary resistance.

That resistance has been real. I have been harassed. My work has been obstructed, ignored, and in some cases deliberately buried. I have faced targeted suppression from people and institutions that benefit from keeping my name out of the record. This is not paranoia — it is a pattern I have lived with for years, one I can prove, and one that threatens to erase contributions that should be celebrated and shared.

The truth is: this is not sustainable without stability. My ability to continue publishing depends on having the most basic foundation — a roof, food, time, and focus. Right now, that foundation is not secure. Every day, I am forced to choose between immediate survival and the research that could genuinely help the world.

If you believe in the work I’ve been doing — if you’ve read my publications, followed my breakthroughs, or seen the vision I’ve laid out — I am asking you to help secure that foundation today.

Here is how you can help right now:

Send a one-time contribution to Venmo: @matthewchenoweth

Share this post so it reaches people who can back it

If you have the means, commit to recurring support so I can focus on the work, not survival

This isn’t just about me. It’s about whether independent, unaligned researchers can still produce and publish important work in a world where information is controlled, filtered, and suppressed. If I can’t maintain stability, the suppression wins — and that means ideas that could benefit all of us remain unseen.

If you’ve ever thought, “This matters,” then I am asking you to act. Now.

— Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Woodland, CA
