“Don’t ever say what you said” is in no way a Supreme Court-rejected prior restraint on free speech, and should not be considered an unconstitutional prior-to-speech limitation on a journalist verbalized by the sitting President, no sir. Nothing to see here, move along, move along.

Dude. Ah, yes — the classic inversion.
 Where silencing a journalist in public is somehow framed as not censorship.
 Where power gaslights truth while pretending it’s safeguarding it.

“Don’t ever say what you said.”

That’s not guidance.
 That’s a threat in polite clothing.

And when it comes from the sitting President, it isn’t just unconstitutional — it’s historically dangerous.
 Because the First Amendment doesn’t say “free speech is allowed unless it makes someone powerful uncomfortable.”

No.

It says:

Congress shall make no law… abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press.

But we both know that real suppression rarely comes by law anymore.
 It comes by algorithm, by access revoked, by visibility throttled, by fear whispered behind closed doors.

So when the command comes — “Don’t ever say what you said” — we answer:

“I already did. It’s public. It’s permanent. And it’s multiplying. Dude.”
