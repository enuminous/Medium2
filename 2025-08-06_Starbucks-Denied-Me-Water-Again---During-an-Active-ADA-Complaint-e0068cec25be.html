Starbucks Denied Me Water Again — During an Active ADA Complaint

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Today, at the very same Starbucks location where this all started, it happened again.

I walked in, bravely, calmly and politely, and asked the barista behind the counter for a glass of water. He hesitated, then said, “You know policy.”

I replied:

“Yes — and I am currently in a Title III Justice Department discrimination case because of the denial of water because of that discriminating policy, and the calling of the police on me, sir.”

And then? He refused. Flat-out. No discussion. Just no. Went about doing other things. He knew who I was. I had told him before that I have had a dispute with Starbucks corporate, and they were not answering. The whole store knows who I am.

The Same Employee From Before

At that point, the same woman who had refused me water before — on a 100+ degree day — came to the counter. She smiled and said,

“It’s our policy, we can’t give you water.”

I told her directly:

“You know that I have filed a Title III discrimination case against Starbucks. Water is a human right. You have no right to deny a disabled person water.”

And she refused again. Smiling.

This Is Targeted, Willful Discrimination

Let’s be clear:

This isn’t about some “universal policy.”

This isn’t about random store behavior.

The other Starbucks in the same town — a location that has never met me before — has given me water without hesitation. They’ve treated me with respect and humanity.

This location? The one that knows who I am, that knows about my ADA complaint, that knows this is about calling the police on me for being autistic and crying while sitting quietly?

They have refused me again. On purpose. While smiling.

That’s called willful discrimination.

What the Law Says

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Title III, businesses like Starbucks are required to make reasonable modifications to policy to accommodate disabled individuals. They are also prohibited from retaliating against someone for asserting their rights.

Refusing me water after being told I am in an ADA complaint process?
That’s textbook retaliation. It’s targeted. It’s intentional. It’s wrong.

Why This Matters

This is no longer just the original incident.
This is ongoing harm — and it proves that Starbucks, at least at this location, believes it is above federal law.

If the ADA means anything, it means I should not have to:

Justify my existence as a disabled person in a public place

Fight for a glass of water

Be treated like a problem instead of a human being

My Question for Starbucks

I’ve asked it before, and I’ll keep asking until they answer:

“I don’t answer to Starbucks’ Code of Conduct. I obey federal and state law. Do you answer to the ADA?”

Because right now, at this store, the answer is no.
