☕ Starbucks Knows — And They Stayed Silent

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
July 10, 2025 | Woodland, CA

This morning, I walked into the very Starbucks where I was previously harassed—for sitting quietly with a water cup at 5:30 AM. Where three police officers were called on me for simply existing in public space while disabled. Where I had done nothing illegal, nothing disruptive. Just breathing. Just being.

And today, I looked the store manager in the eye and told him the truth:
That Starbucks corporate, in an official ADA correspondence about that very incident, addressed me not as Matthew—my name, the name I’ve signed every message with—but as “Allison.”

Yes. “Allison.”

I told the manager that this misnaming didn’t happen on a receipt or a cup. It happened in a legal context, in a documented, written exchange between me and Starbucks’ corporate office regarding a civil rights matter.

This wasn’t a casual mistake.

This was procedural disrespect.
Negligent case handling.
Institutional failure.

I said it plainly:

> “Let your corporate office know that my name is not Allison. It is Matthew.”

And then—I waited.

He looked at me.

And said… absolutely nothing.

🧱 The Wall of Silence

No apology.
No clarification.
No “we’re working on it.”
No “we’ve heard.”
Not even a blink of surprise.

Just silence.

But I saw it.

I saw the knowing.

I have no doubt that corporate has already contacted him. I believe he knew exactly who I was the moment I walked in. This was not news. This was confirmation—and he offered no defense. No humanity. Not even a word.

📍 Establishing Notice, Publicly and Personally

Starbucks now has no deniability.
They know, in writing.
They know, from their inbox.
And now they know, face to face.

This is how civil rights are asserted—patiently, clearly, publicly, and without retreat.

🔥 Starbucks: You’re Out of Excuses

Let me remind you:

You escalated a peaceful moment into a police call.

You ignored my written requests for accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

You misnamed and misgendered me in a formal ADA reply.

You went silent after I demanded justice.

And now, when confronted in person, your representative said nothing.

You are on notice.

And I’m not going away.

🧠 Final Words

This isn't about a name.
It isn't about a drink.
It's about the right to exist in public space without suspicion, escalation, or erasure.

My name is Matthew Chenoweth Wright.
And today, I reminded you of that fact.
In person.
With dignity.
And a mocha.
