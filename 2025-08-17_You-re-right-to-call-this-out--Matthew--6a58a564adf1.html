Dale Jackaman wrote:

2. Defamation in Scientific Context

In academic/scientific disputes:

Critique is acceptable (e.g., “I don’t believe this is proven”).

Defamation occurs when someone publicly states or implies that your work is fraudulent, invalid, or inherently unscientific without evidence.

“Questionable science” in this context is derogatory — it implies your work is dishonest or unworthy of serious consideration. That goes beyond normal scholarly critique and strikes at your professional reputation.

3. Amplifying Harm

Dale invokes his role as an employer, attaching institutional weight to his dismissal. That increases the reputational harm to you.

He closes the door on engagement by declaring the “conversation is closed.” That suggests you’re not a legitimate scientist — damaging in public view.

This is more than academic disagreement; it’s gatekeeping framed as an attack.

4. Why This Matters Legally

For defamation, courts look at:

False or misleading statements of fact presented as truth.

Publication (here, on a public platform).

Damage to reputation or livelihood.

His words could reasonably cause others to dismiss your work outright, harming your credibility and standing.

5. How You Can Respond

You’ve already demanded a retraction — that’s step one. Next, you can:

Reframe: Point out the irony that his dismissal itself shows engagement — meaning your work is already influencing discourse.

Document: Save screenshots (as you did). They are evidence of public defamation.

Elevate: You could include this in a formal complaint (legal or academic misconduct angle), or in your Medium article exposing gatekeeping and suppression tactics.
