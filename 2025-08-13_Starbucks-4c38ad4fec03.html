The Coffee Chain That Calls the Cops on Thirst, Bladders — and Now, Disability

It sounds absurd until it happens to you. You walk into Starbucks, a paying customer. You buy your drink, maybe a snack. Midday, you need to use the restroom — a basic human right in any civilized space — only to find it blocked by cones. No “out of order” sign. No staff inside. Just a silent barrier.

You ask politely. You’re told no.
You explain you urgently need to go. Still no.
So you do what any reasonable person would: you step past the cones and into the restroom.

And then, the escalation — they call the police.

Yes, you read that right: at Starbucks, using a restroom you’ve already helped pay for with your purchase can apparently be treated as a criminal act. And it’s not just me. A friend of mine — we’ll call her C. — has witnessed the same kind of treatment: denied access to basic facilities, made to feel unwelcome in a place where she was the customer.

A Pattern of Exclusion

These incidents are not isolated. Starbucks is now facing a Title III action under the Americans with Disabilities Act — and just yesterday, I personally experienced another example of exclusion that makes the pattern impossible to ignore.

I was seated in the outdoor area, trying to collect myself after a disability-induced panic attack. My goal was simple: to regulate my breathing, restore some composure, and then continue with my day. Instead, an employee approached me and told me to leave. I explained — clearly and directly — that I was in distress because of a medical condition. The statement was ignored. My vulnerability was met not with compassion, but with removal.

The Culture Problem

This is bigger than a few rude employees. Starbucks has a documented history of calling the police on customers over minor or imagined infractions — from a high-profile Philadelphia case involving two Black men waiting for a meeting, to countless lower-profile incidents that never make national news. Now, “needing to drink water,” “needing to use the restroom,” or “needing a moment to recover from a disability event” can apparently be added to the list of behaviors treated as cause for police involvement or ejection.

Weaponizing law enforcement and exclusionary policy against basic human needs is not customer service. It’s a systemic cultural problem — one that sees certain bodies, needs, and conditions as inconvenient disruptions to be removed rather than respected.

The Human Cost

The physical impact of being denied a restroom or water is immediate. The emotional impact — of being treated like a criminal for a bodily function, or like a trespasser for managing a disability — lingers long after. Incidents like these create scars. They chip away at dignity. They send a message: you are not welcome here.

That’s not just bad PR. That’s actionable harm.

Maybe Starbucks should consider a new slogan:
“Come for the coffee. Stay out of the restroom. Don’t sit too long. Or else.”
