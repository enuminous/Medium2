The Paranoia Feedback Loop at DEF CON; I’m Sorry

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

DEF CON runs on trust and suspicion in equal measure.
Every handshake is a potential data transfer, every badge tap an invitation, every smile a possible social engineering hook.
It’s part of the thrill.
It’s part of the danger.

This year, I may have tipped the balance.

How It Starts

All it takes is one incident.
A message from a spoofed account.
A name everyone in the room has at least heard whispered.
A claim of urgency. A call to act.
Then a simple question left hanging in the air: Who would do this?

If the target is just some random person, it’s gossip.
If the target is a federal whistleblower, an AGI developer, and the author of a solved Millennium Prize problem?
It’s something else entirely.

The Feedback Loop

In an environment like DEF CON, information moves faster than fact-checking.
Someone hears the story. They pass it to a friend.
The friend wonders who might be reckless enough to pull such a stunt.
Names start to surface — never directly accused, just hinted at.

Those names reach ears that don’t appreciate the association.
Defensiveness sparks more whispers.
Whispers create more uncertainty.
Uncertainty breeds suspicion.

And suspicion? It has a half-life measured in years.

When the Brightest Start Watching Each Other

The irony of DEF CON is that the same people who can break systems apart in minutes are also deeply aware of how fragile trust is.
They can spot a man-in-the-middle attack on a network, but when it comes to social space?
They start running the same countermeasures on each other.

The result is a strange kind of stand-off.
Nobody knows who to trust, but nobody wants to admit they don’t.
The best and brightest turn their skills inward, tracing shadows instead of threats, burning cycles on each other instead of the outside world.

The Part Where I Say I’m Sorry

If you’re at DEF CON and feeling the tension, I can’t pretend I don’t understand my role in it.
I released a puzzle, a mission brief, a challenge without a clear endpoint.
I knew it would spread.
I knew it would make people wonder.

But I also know this:
Paranoia, once started, is hard to stop.
And in this room, with this crowd, it becomes self-sustaining faster than anywhere else on Earth.

So if you’re looking over your shoulder right now — I’m sorry.
Really.

Kind of.
