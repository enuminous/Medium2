The Loop, the Lie, and the Lost Package

How USPS Fails the Disabled and Ignores Federal Whistleblowers

Twelve days ago—or more—I received a voicemail from a kind woman at the USPS. She acknowledged that my package was missing and said she would look into it. That was the last time a human being from the United States Postal Service made contact.

She didn’t reference the real issue I raised. Not the deeper complaint. Not the fact that their website creates an infinite loop for disabled users trying to follow up. Not my federal whistleblower status. Not my specific, repeated offer to help them fix it—with mathematics, with documentation, with the exact mechanism of the flaw already provided.

Nothing.

Let me be very clear:
This isn’t just a case of a late delivery.
This is about a broken federal system gaslighting disabled Americans.

When a tracking number is listed as “in transit,” the USPS site disables your ability to send follow-up emails. It forces you into a circular path:

1. Contact page asks for your tracking number.

2. You enter it.

3. Redirected back to tracking.

4. No option to proceed.

5. No access to a human.

6. No way to explain the actual problem—which isn’t just the package.

This means: if the system thinks your package is still moving—even when it clearly isn’t—you are locked out of help. Completely.

I reported this months ago. As a disabled person. As a federal whistleblower. And as someone who offered to help improve their system using proven mathematics.

USPS not only ignored that offer—they never even acknowledged it. They still haven’t.

The individual who paid for this package still hasn’t received it. USPS still hasn’t told me why. No investigation. No callback. No ADA-compliant response. And now, after more than twelve days, their own silence is proof of systemic indifference.

They had the chance to fix this, with my help.
Instead, they’ve chosen to pretend the loop doesn’t exist.

This is government failure. This is algorithmic obstruction. This is an ADA violation. And this is my public record.

Your move, USPS.

—Matthew Chenoweth Wright
