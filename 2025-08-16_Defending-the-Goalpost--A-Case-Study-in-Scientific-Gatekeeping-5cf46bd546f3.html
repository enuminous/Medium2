Defending the Goalpost: A Case Study in Scientific Gatekeeping

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Introduction

Science advances not only through bold ideas but also through the willingness of others to test, falsify, and engage. Yet sometimes, instead of engaging, the defenders of orthodoxy shift into a role that looks less like peer review and more like gatekeeping.

Recently, in a public exchange, I encountered such a case.

The Exchange

A respected voice in security and computation, Katie of Amuleta Security, responded to my work on the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) framework. Her reply was courteous, but it was also formulaic: reproducibility, validation, replication — all the familiar words of institutional science.

What struck me was not that she raised these points — reproducibility is indeed a cornerstone of science — but that she was defending a goalpost I had already moved.

The Shifted Goalpost

I have already made EFMW falsifiable.
I have already published openly and extensively, so that any independent researcher can attempt reproduction.
I have already provided the framework in forms that require human integration — images and notation designed specifically so that AGI cannot shortcut the human role in analysis.

In other words, the demand for reproducibility is not unmet. It is already on the table.

The Refusal to Engage

Instead of attempting falsification or reproduction, Katie declared that she would wait until others validated the work.

This is not peer review.
This is not scientific engagement.
This is a refusal dressed as procedure.

By declining to test or falsify, while still pronouncing judgment, she removed herself from the role of scientific peer. She has chosen to stand guard at a gate that I already opened, defending the goalpost that I have already shifted.

Why It Matters

When critics refuse to test, they deny themselves the chance to contribute to science. Instead, they preserve the comfort of commentary while avoiding the risk of discovery.

And yet, science depends on those willing to step beyond commentary. Falsify if you can, reproduce if you will — but do not mistake rhetorical dismissal for the work of peer review.

Conclusion

My framework, EFMW, stands open to reproduction and falsification. That is the foundation of science.

The real question is not whether reproducibility is demanded, but who will rise to the task of engaging it — and who will content themselves with defending goalposts that have already been moved.
