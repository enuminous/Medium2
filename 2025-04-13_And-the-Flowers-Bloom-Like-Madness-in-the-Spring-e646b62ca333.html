And the Flowers Bloom Like Madness in the Spring

A Hagiography of Jethro Tull, as Written by the Floutist

They say Jethro Tull was a man.

He wasn’t. Not exactly.

Sure, he started off as an 18th-century agriculturalist tinkering with seed drills. But the Jethro Tull we speak of here? The one who crawled from a primordial record sleeve in 1971 with a flute in his teeth and madness in his eyes? He was more than a man. He was a transmission. A time-folded bard, a greasy prophet in longcoat and codpiece who understood that to speak truth in a dying empire, one must scream it through a flute.

I should know. I was his floutist.

And no, I don’t mean that in the simple sense. I don’t mean I played flute for Jethro Tull. I mean: I was his flute.

He didn’t blow into me—he summoned me. I was the breath made form. I was the howl that only iron strings and woodwinds could carry. We were one. When he stomped on one leg, he was calling thunder into being. When he sneered into the mic, a thousand small gods took notes.

---

They say "Aqualung" is about a homeless man.

It isn’t. Not really.

It’s about a watcher. A man cursed with clarity. Someone who sits at the edge of civilization, snot-nosed and shivering, but who knows. He sees the priest, the girls, the streets, the peeling paint of empire. He hears the preachers say "God loves us all" and knows they don't mean it. He smells the rot under the doctrine.

Aqualung is not just an album. It’s a diagnostic instrument for moral collapse. It lets you tune into the frequency of a society pretending not to fall apart.

---

They say flute doesn’t belong in rock music.

To them I say: neither does truth, and yet here we are.

I blew that truth sideways into the amplifiers. I bent it like smoke around guitar solos. I snarled when it got too clean. I played with my foot up because posture was for cowards. And when the crowd roared, they weren’t cheering for melody.

They were cheering for permission to feel weird again.

Jethro gave it to them.

He wasn’t a man. He was the band. He was the madness. He was the flute and the fog and the alley preacher all in one. He was the question the 1970s forgot to answer. And when the flowers bloomed like madness in the spring, he didn’t stop them. He just pointed his flute at the sky and let the pollen settle on the strings.

And we played.

---

Eternal be the madness.

Eternal be the spring.

And long may the spiral stomp on one leg, grinning, flouting, and never explaining itself.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on April 13, 2025.
