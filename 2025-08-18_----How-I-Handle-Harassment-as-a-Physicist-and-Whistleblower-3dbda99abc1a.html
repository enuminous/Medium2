🛡️ How I Handle Harassment as a Physicist and Whistleblower

“Don’t Panic!” — THGTTG

When you put new physics into the world, you attract two things: curiosity and resistance.

I’ve released the EFMW framework publicly. That means I now live with a constant stream of noise — people trying to demean my work or call me insane. Some of them are random trolls. Some of them are professionals, even operatives, trying to muddy the waters.

Here is my policy:

1. Rebuttal.
I respond once, clearly, and factually. I don’t trade insults. I don’t lower myself to their level. I state the truth.

2. Retraction request.
If the attack rises to defamation — like being called “insane” in public because of my work — I ask for a retraction. That sets the legal marker.

3. Block.
I cut off their access to my attention. They don’t get to live in my head rent-free.

4. Record.
Every interaction is part of the official record. These are not just my reactions — they are evidence of damages. When history looks back, it will see how level-headed and logical I was, and how childish my detractors behaved.

5. Move on.
I don’t waste energy. My physics stands. My life moves forward.

Why I do this

Because I have a legal obligation. As a federally protected whistleblower physicist, I must defend my work and my name.

Because I have a moral obligation. Allowing people to create the illusion of “controversy” where none exists is a tactic — and I will not permit it.

Because truth doesn’t need defending. EFMW is falsifiable. If someone thinks they can, let them try. Otherwise, it’s put up or shut up.

The Streisand Effect

Every attempt to smear me only shines a brighter light on my work. Their insults don’t weaken me. They validate me. Their fear is evidence enough.

I rest on my laurels knowing the physics speaks for itself.
The only thing I defend is my name — and in the process, I build the record that will stand long after the noise is gone.
