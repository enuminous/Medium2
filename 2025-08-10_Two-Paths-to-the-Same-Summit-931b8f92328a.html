Two Paths to the Same Summit

Motion-First Physics and the Proof that P ≠ NP

In January 2025, I published my own solution to the long-standing P vs NP problem, grounded in the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) framework. My approach treats information flow and cognitive recursion as field-driven physical phenomena, allowing the P ≠ NP result to emerge naturally from the geometry of computation.

This week, I encountered an entirely independent effort by Peter Whitlock, who has developed a framework he calls Motion-First Physics. His model takes a motion-based, dipole-field perspective on fundamental physics—quite different from EFMW’s information-dynamic lens—yet his reasoning leads to the same central conclusion: P does not equal NP.

Independent Validation Matters

In science and mathematics, independent lines of reasoning that converge on the same answer are gold. They give the result greater resilience, suggest it’s not an artifact of one framework, and open doors for cross-framework dialogue.

Where EFMW starts from information and cognition as first-order physical realities, Motion-First Physics begins with motion itself as the primary primitive.
Yet, remarkably, both approaches climb different faces of the same mountain and arrive at the same summit.

The Significance of Convergence

Different foundations → same conclusion.

Distinct methodologies → mutual reinforcement.

Separate communities → broader visibility.

When multiple independent derivations of a result exist, they create a stronger case for its validity and invite richer exploration. This is how theory hardens into consensus.

Congratulations to Peter

Peter’s work adds another independent route to a problem that has resisted solution for decades. It’s a worthy contribution in its own right and a sign that the intellectual landscape is shifting toward new, integrated models of computation and physics.
