I Wasn’t in an Altercation — I Was Autistic and Crying

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Let’s be honest about what happened.

I wasn’t disruptive. I wasn’t hostile. I wasn’t aggressive.

I was crying.

Quietly. Softly. Sitting at a Starbucks table with a cup of water, trying to hold myself together in public because I didn’t have anywhere else to go.

I’m autistic. That means I feel things deeply, sometimes too deeply for the world’s pace. And when it becomes too much — when grief or pressure or injustice reaches its edge — I cry. Not to manipulate. Not to provoke. Just to survive the wave.

But instead of being treated like a human being, I was treated like a threat.

The store manager approached me, asked me to leave. I explained my disability. I asked to be allowed to sit, to calm down, to breathe. She told me I could stay just this once.

But when I quietly, respectfully tried to explain how much this hurt — how it felt to be made into a problem for being autistic and in distress — her tone shifted.

Suddenly, I was causing an altercation.

Those were her exact words. Me speaking my truth, me asking to be allowed to grieve with dignity — that became an “altercation.”

She rescinded her offer.

She said she’d call security if I didn’t leave.

And when I started crying more — not yelling, not making threats, not raising my voice — she called the police.

Let me be clear:

Labeling an autistic person’s attempt to self-advocate and express emotional distress as an “altercation” is not only false — it is dangerous.

It’s how institutions convert vulnerability into justification for removal. It’s how feelings become threats. It’s how crying becomes criminal.

When the police arrived, they could see what I was: a person in pain. Not a criminal. Not a danger. Not a threat. Just someone trying to get through the morning with his dignity intact.

They let me go.

But the damage was already done.

Because a manager with too much authority and too little empathy decided that my tears were a violation of policy. Because my existence didn’t match her comfort. Because my autism — and my truth — became a liability for her brand.

I was labeled. I was erased. I was nearly criminalized for grieving too visibly.

I wasn’t in an altercation.

I was in mourning. I was in distress. I was human.

And if Starbucks wants to be a place of inclusion, they need to understand that inclusion means more than race and rainbow flags. It means recognizing the invisible — the neurodivergent, the grieving, the tired, the overstimulated, the quietly falling apart.

Because until they do?

Every tear is a risk. Every word is a weapon in someone else’s narrative. And every cry becomes a charge.

I wasn’t in an altercation.

I was autistic and crying.

And that should never require an apology.

— Matthew Chenoweth Wright
