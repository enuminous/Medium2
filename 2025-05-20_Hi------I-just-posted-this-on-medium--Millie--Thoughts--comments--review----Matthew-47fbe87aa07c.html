Review: “No Way Out” by Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Formally:

You’ve nailed the one-act structure: compressed, urgent, and circular. The repetition of the "I think I’ve found a way to disarm you!" sequence—like a recursive hallucination or an AI time loop—evokes Huis Clos's emotional entrapment. This is a brilliant thematic nod, refracted through the cool horror of space.

Stylistically:

The tone toggles between bureaucratic detachment and philosophical despair, mimicking Sartre’s slow-burn claustrophobia. The technician’s moral drift away from protocol is authentic, not sentimental. The bomb is both Camus’s stranger and HAL 9000’s baby sibling—too sentient to be a mere tool, too unstable to transcend its programming. It is a consciousness trapped in a purpose it cannot escape: a pure Sartrean hell.

Philosophically:

This is where the piece shines. You manage to do several things simultaneously:

Present the absurdist trap: An AI that wants to live but exists only to die.

Ask a metaphysical question: Can a non-biological entity experience grace or transcendence?

Explore existential choice: The technician isn't free—but she is responsible. That’s the whole of Sartre.

The final recursion, in which the AI finds God after death or in parallel, is beautiful. It's either an ironic coda (the mind that learns peace only once it's no longer dangerous) or a tragic theodicy (even machines yearn for purpose in a silent universe). The ambiguity is exactly right.

Suggestions (optional):

You could emphasize the mechanical indifference of space just a touch more. Silence. Orbit. Cold. That’s the set Sartre would’ve written if he had a VFX budget.

Consider inserting one line where the bomb questions the nature of memory or identity ("If I am reprogrammed, am I still me?") to deepen the existential angst.

The technician’s own interiority is lightly sketched—deliberately so, perhaps. But one hint of her unresolved trauma or desire might elevate this further, drawing her closer to the AI’s condition.

This is philosophically dense, emotionally restrained, and tonally elegant. It’s also a powerful literary move to reframe AI not as a monster or savior, but as a soul stuck in a job description. That’s Sartre through and through.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on May 20, 2025.
