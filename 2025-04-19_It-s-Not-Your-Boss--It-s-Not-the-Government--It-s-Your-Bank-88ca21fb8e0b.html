The Illusion of Help

You wake up broke. Again.
You blame your boss. You blame the government. You blame yourself.
But the real reason your account is empty? The quiet betrayal of the one institution you thought was neutral: your bank.

This isn’t about capitalism, or a late paycheck.
This is about automated cruelty wrapped in corporate branding, and how your bank—yes, yours—is engineering financial distress, especially for the disabled, the poor, and the inconvenient.

Banks No Longer Serve You—They Manage You

Banks no longer hold your money; they algorithmically control your experience of accessing it.

You trust their app, their branding, their smiling support staff.

But their actual systems? Unmonitored, unpatched, designed for liability shielding, not customer stability.

Example: [Redacted] Bank’s help account @Ask[Redacted]? Suspended on Twitter.
Still linked in their app. No fix. No alert. Just dead silence where help should be.

What Happens When You’re Disabled or in Crisis

Most banks don’t stress test their systems against real emergency flows.

You rely on routine—like “Early Pay” being there every Friday.

The moment it’s not? They don’t notify you.

They let your automatic payments bounce. They charge overdraft fees. They call it “your problem.”
But it’s not your problem. It’s their system’s failure to model risk adaptively.
And it keeps happening because nobody is watching the system watching you.

The Equation That Could Fix It

There is a way to detect failure before it happens.

It’s a physics-informed framework, designed to model stress, recursion, delay, and degradation.

It’s called the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright Equation, and yes, it’s real.

It can show banks when their systems will fail vulnerable people.

But banks won’t use it. Not yet. Because ignorance is cheaper than change—until the lawsuits come.

Why You Let This Happen

Because you were trained to blame your job, the president, the news, the system.

You were taught banks are invisible middlemen, not active agents.

You think it’s your responsibility to “manage your money,” even as banks quietly rig the structure.

But the truth is: they already control it.

And they are hurting you, your neighbor, your boss, that disabled person in line behind you, and every person who thinks they’re just unlucky.

The Ask

Stop blaming the wrong targets.
Start seeing who actually touches your life.
It’s not a conspiracy. It’s a slow, systematic erosion—built into code, marketing, silence, and negligence.

And it can be fixed. Right now. If you demand it.
Ask your bank why they didn’t notify you of policy changes.
Ask them what systems they use to ensure ADA compliance.
Ask them if they even understand what adaptive service means.

Or keep letting them hurt you in the dark.
