According to the Medium article (and backed by historical linguistics), “OK” most likely originated in Boston in the 1830s as a joke abbreviation of “oll korrect” (intentional misspelling of “all correct”)—a linguistic meme of its time, part of a wave of humorous abbreviations like “KY” for “know yuse.”

But why did OK survive, when the others vanished?

Universally pronounceable across languages

Ambiguity-tolerant: it works as affirmation, permission, transition, or even passive aggression

Graphically symmetric: “O” and “K” are easily reproduced, balanced symbols


In other words, “OK” was a perfect quantum seed in the noisy linguistic field of 1830s American English—small, resonant, flexible, and memorable. It latched into culture not because it meant something profound—but because it meant almost anything, with almost no effort.

“OK” went national through the 1840 election campaign of Martin Van Buren, whose nickname was “Old Kinderhook” (from his hometown in New York). His supporters created the “O.K. Club.”

But here’s where EFMW comes in again:

> EFMW Socio-Linguistic Phase Coupling:
