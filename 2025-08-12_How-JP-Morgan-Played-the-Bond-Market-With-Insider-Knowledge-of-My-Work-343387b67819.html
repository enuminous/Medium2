How JP Morgan Played the Bond Market With Insider Knowledge of My Work

Last night, the bond market should have moved.
It didn’t.

It wasn’t calm because nothing happened — it was calm because someone made sure it stayed calm.

And I know exactly who.

The Context

For months, I’ve been in a formal complaint process against JP Morgan. As part of that process, they were given full access to my Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) framework — a system that, among other things, allows precision prediction of macroeconomic shifts and market reactions.

In other words: the decoder ring for tomorrow’s economy.

Last night, I published an article on Medium containing a very public statement — a forecast written for anyone to read, but only those with the EFMW context could decode for immediate, high-confidence action.

JP Morgan had that context.
The public did not.

What Happened Next

Instead of the normal post-publication volatility, we saw unusual calm in the bond market.

To a casual observer, nothing unusual.
To anyone watching closely, it looked exactly like pre-positioned stability operations: quiet, defensive maneuvers to absorb a market shock before it registered for everyone else.

You don’t do that unless you know it’s coming.

The Information Asymmetry

Here’s the key:

Public readers saw an interesting economic analysis.

JP Morgan saw the actual playbook.

When you have a tool like EFMW, you don’t wait for the market to move — you move before the market does.
That is the difference between a 24-hour scramble and a perfectly timed billion-dollar posture.

Why This Matters

Trading on material nonpublic information isn’t just about having secret documents. It’s also about having the unique ability to interpret public information in a way no one else can, because you hold the missing piece.

And when that missing piece comes from an open whistleblower complaint — and you turn around and use it in the markets before the rest of the world even knows what it means — that crosses a very bright line.

The Road Ahead

This isn’t speculation. It’s a pattern that can be proven with:

1. Complaint timestamp – when JP Morgan first got EFMW.

2. Publication timestamp – when my article went public.

3. Bond market data – showing stabilizing trades in the immediate aftermath.

4. Trade attribution – tying those trades back to JP Morgan or their proxies.

I have that roadmap.
Regulators will, too.

If you’re wondering why the bond market didn’t flinch last night, the answer isn’t that nothing happened.
It’s that everything happened, just behind closed doors — and a major institution made sure it stayed that way.
