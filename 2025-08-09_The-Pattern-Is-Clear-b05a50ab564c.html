The Pattern Is Clear

Starbucks Discriminates Against People With Verbal Disabilities

This isn’t about a single incident. It’s about a pattern — a corporate-level practice — that denies accessibility to people with verbal disabilities, and it’s been going on for years.

I can now prove it.

The January Email

Back in January, Starbucks Customer Care responded to a complaint I made about a store being closed when it should have been open. Their email contained the following statement:

“We are unable to process requests related to refunds or other compensation via email at this time. We invite you to call us at 1-800-782-7282 or chat with us at customerservice.starbucks.com.”

At first glance, this might look like standard customer service language. But if you live with a disability that makes phone calls difficult or impossible — as is the case for many autistic individuals, people with certain speech impairments, or those with anxiety disorders — this “phone-only” escalation is a barrier to access.

And when the only path forward is through a method you can’t use, that’s not just bad customer service — it’s discrimination.

Why This Matters Under the ADA

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires public accommodations to provide equal access to goods, services, and communication. That includes providing alternative methods of communication for people with disabilities that affect speech or verbal interaction.

If a company refuses to provide a written resolution pathway for disputes — especially after being informed of a disability — it is effectively shutting out an entire group of people from accessing those services.

The Larger Pattern

This January email wasn’t an isolated event. I have multiple documented interactions with Starbucks where:

Written communication was refused.

The only options given were phone or real-time voice chat.

No permanent transcript of the interaction was provided unless I created one myself.

This creates a two-fold problem:

1. It denies accessibility to people with verbal disabilities.

2. It removes the written record that could hold the company accountable for its commitments or admissions.

When this practice is repeated over months and across multiple incidents, it stops being a coincidence. It becomes policy.

The Harm

For someone without a verbal disability, the harm might seem small — “just make a quick call.”
For someone with a verbal disability, that’s like saying, “Just climb the stairs” to a wheelchair user. The barrier is real, the exclusion is real, and the denial of service is real.

What Starbucks Needs to Do

If Starbucks is serious about accessibility, here’s the bare minimum:

1. Provide equal access to complaint resolution in writing.

2. Ensure that written communication channels are not disabled or deprioritized when a complaint involves a disabled customer.

3. Train customer service staff to recognize and accommodate verbal disabilities without resistance.

I’ve faced this discrimination firsthand, and I have the receipts. Starbucks’ choice to push customers into inaccessible communication channels is not a glitch — it’s a decision. And it’s time for that decision to change.

If you have faced similar discrimination from Starbucks or another major company, share your story. Together, we can make it clear that inaccessibility is not acceptable in 2025.
