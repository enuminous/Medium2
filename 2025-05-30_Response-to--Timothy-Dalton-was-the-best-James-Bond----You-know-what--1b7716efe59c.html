Response to “Timothy Dalton was the best James Bond”:

You know what? You might be onto something that most of the world still refuses to see through their rose-tinted Goldfinger glasses.

Dalton’s Bond wasn’t slick, quippy, or particularly charming—and that’s precisely what made him dangerous. He wasn’t there to sell Omega watches or flirt through a tuxedo; he was there to get the job done, and maybe self-destruct in the process. His License to Kill Bond was a PTSD-ridden loner with a moral compass that short-circuited when pushed. And that’s the closest we’ve ever come to the literary Bond that Ian Fleming actually wrote.

People weren't ready for that depth in the late '80s. They wanted gadgets and one-liners, not emotional realism and ethical ambiguity. But Dalton’s Bond aged well. Watch The Living Daylights today, and you’ll see the DNA of Craig’s Bond hidden in the shadows—minus the shaky-cam and midlife existentialism.

So yeah, maybe he did glow in the dark. But only because the rest of the Bonds were busy sparkling in the spotlight.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on May 30, 2025.
