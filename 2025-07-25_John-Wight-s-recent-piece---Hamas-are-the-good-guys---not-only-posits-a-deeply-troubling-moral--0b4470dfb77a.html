John Wight’s recent piece, “Hamas are the good guys”, not only posits a deeply troubling moral inversion—it does so with sweeping rhetoric and historical erasure. His framing stands firmly on the wrong side of history.

🧾 How His Argument Fails the Ethical & Historical Test

1. Selective Narration & Moral Blindness

Wight opens with a quote from Frantz Fanon, projecting the Palestinian resistance as an existential defiance of colonial oppression. Yet he omits mention of Hamas’s longstanding use of civilian violence, child combatants, and antisemitic propaganda—all well-documented in both its 1988 charter and 2017 revision .

2. Romanticizing Reactive Violence

He lauds Hamas’s October 7 attack as a symbolic triumph—“ingenious” and defiant. What he omits: nearly 1,200 civilians killed and mass kidnappings—widely condemned by international law and legal experts as war crimes or genocide-level atrocities .

3. Historical Miscontextualization

Frantz Fanon’s critique of colonial dehumanization is twisted into an endorsement of terrorism. Wight transforms defiance into virtue, ignoring that Fanon's rejection of colonialism was not carte blanche for the indiscriminate killing of civilians.

🧠 EFMW Field Integrity Analysis

Using the EFMW framework—Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright recursive ethics—we can parse Wight’s narrative as a symbolic cascade lacking recursive coherence and moral endpoint:

EFMW Axis Observed Defect

E (Emergence) Hamas is portrayed as arising organically. But real emergence demands ethical recursion, which is missing.
F (Feedback) No feedback loops present. Only amplification of pro-violence rhetoric.
M (Motion) Rhetoric flows from grievance to glorification without transformation or system-level reform.
W (Wonder) No wonder. Only binary oppression framing—colonizer vs. oppressed—serves as just justification for violence.

A truly recursive ethics discourse would question the costs of identity-bound violence, not romanticize them.

📌 Why This Is Dangerous

Whitewashing terrorism: Declaring Hamas “good” ignores their use of terror, antisemitic language in earlier charters, and denial of Israel's right to exist .

Eliding civilian suffering: The victims of extremist tactics—children, journalists, small villages—are erased from his narrative.

Propaganda-style simplification: Wight repeats the binary myth: oppressor vs. oppressed = moral absolution. This is ideological propaganda, not historical analysis.

🧾 In Summary

John Wight’s essay isn't a radical reframe—it’s a moral inversion. It elevates Hamas through selective omission and emotional posturing, all while ignoring documented human rights abuses and antisemitic violence.

📌 In modern discourse, claiming “Hamas are the good guys” is both factually inaccurate and morally indefensible. It marks not a courageous dissent, but a rejection of any semblance of ethical nuance.
