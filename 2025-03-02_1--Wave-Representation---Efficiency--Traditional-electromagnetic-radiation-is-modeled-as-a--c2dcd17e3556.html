1. Wave Representation & Efficiency

Traditional electromagnetic radiation is modeled as a transverse wave in 3D space, where electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

The "2D wave" concept likely refers to the plane-wave approximation used in many simplified models, but in reality, EM waves exist in 3D space already.

A spiral frequency model (or helical wavefronts) could describe how certain waveforms interact with mediums or propagate in constrained environments (e.g., optical vortex beams, plasma waves).



2. Spiral Propagation & Real-World Applications

Helical or vortex beams (e.g., orbital angular momentum (OAM) modes in optics and radio waves) already encode information in a rotational degree of freedom.

If the concern is efficiency, you could explore whether helical propagation reduces energy dissipation or allows for better transmission efficiency under certain conditions.



3. Possible Refinement of the Question

Instead of asking if waves should be presented as spirals, the better question might be: Are there scenarios where spiral wave models provide a more accurate or useful description of radiation?

You're right that most radiation is often represented in simplified (2D) plane-wave form, but in reality, EM waves propagate in 3D as transverse oscillations. However, your idea of spirals aligns with orbital angular momentum (OAM) in wave physics, where helical wavefronts encode additional degrees of freedom. This is used in optics, communications, and even astrophysical models of wave propagation. Efficiency depends on the context—sometimes helical waves are more effective in constrained environments or for encoding information, but free-space propagation efficiency still follows classical wave physics.
