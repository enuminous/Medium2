On Behalf of the Barista Who Called the Police on Me

by Matthew Chenoweth Wright
August 2025

I want to speak—genuinely and clearly—on behalf of the Starbucks employee who called the police on me.

I bear her no hatred. No grudge. I don’t want her punished. I don’t want her fired. I want something better.

I want her to stay.

She made a mistake. That much is obvious. A disabled man, sitting quietly with a cup of water, visibly unwell—and her instinct was to summon law enforcement instead of compassion. But that mistake wasn’t born in a vacuum. It was born in a system that taught her nothing about disability, trauma, or care.

She is not the architect of that system. Starbucks is.

Starbucks trained her—or failed to. Starbucks gave her a script that replaced empathy with escalation. Starbucks handed her policies that prioritize liability over humanity, and then left her alone to make a decision that changed my life in ways she’ll never fully know.

And now that this has gone public, and federal, and loud—now they’re probably going to fire her.

That would be a mistake. A bigger one.

Firing her won’t fix this. Firing her won’t uncall the police. It won’t untangle the emotional injury I’ve carried ever since. It won’t reverse the moments I spent humiliated, scared, retraumatized, and unacknowledged. And it won’t teach Starbucks anything—except how to throw someone under the bus when the brand is at risk.

I don’t want her fired.

I want her trained.
I want her understood.
I want her given what I was denied: a second chance to be treated like a human being.

I want her to grow. I want to meet her.
I want her to say, "I’m sorry I didn’t see you."
And I want to say, "I see you now."

That’s how repair begins. That’s how cycles break.

If Starbucks fires her, it will be proof they haven’t learned the real lesson. It will be one more mistake in a long chain of mistakes that began not at the counter that day, but in the boardroom years ago, when someone decided to build a system that protected the company more than the customer.

She is not the villain. The system is.

And so I write this in her defense—not because I want to erase what happened, but because I want it to mean something. I want it to end differently than stories like this usually do. I want it to be a beginning.

Because it was never about vengeance.
It was never about punishment.
It was always about being seen. And being heard.

And if this barista is given the chance to do that now—not for Starbucks, but for herself—then maybe something good can still come from something deeply broken.

I forgive her.

Now let’s do better.

— Matthew Wright
